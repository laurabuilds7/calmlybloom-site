Let's be honest

Crypto feels scary. And honestly? That's completely valid.

I am not going to pretend the reasons people stay out of this world aren't real. They are. So before we talk about what's possible, here are the three walls I see people hit, and what we do about each of them.

01

The complexity is overwhelming.

Blockchains, wallets, gas fees, seed phrases. Most people feel lost before they've even started, and that's not a failing — it's a vocabulary that was never built with you in mind.

02

The volatility is genuinely frightening.

Up 40%, down 60%, up again overnight. Without a real plan and someone calmer than the market guiding you, it feels like a rollercoaster with no seatbelt.

03

The scams have made trust nearly impossible.

Everyone's promising the moon and disappearing with the money. The mistrust isn't paranoia — it's a rational response to a space that has badly let people down.

You don't need to become a crypto expert. You just need to walk in with someone who already is.

What this is, and isn't

Not another crypto pitch.

No signals. No tips. No "just trust me" tables of green numbers. No guarantees of any returns, ever. This isn't a trading service and I'm not a licensed financial advisor.

What I am is someone who walked into crypto as a complete outsider, asked a lot of beginner questions, made a lot of small mistakes, and slowly built a relationship with it that feels manageable. Not exciting. Not dramatic. Manageable. That's the part nobody seems to talk about.

If you're curious about crypto but every resource you've opened so far has felt like it was written by a 22-year-old shouting from a Lamborghini, this is the opposite of that.

The free call

What a 30-minute conversation looks like.

The call is free, it's on Zoom or WhatsApp, no slides, no pitch deck. Here's what happens, start to finish.

  1. You tell me where you're at.

    What's got you curious. What's making you hesitant. What you've already tried, if anything.

  2. I ask more questions than I answer.

    The first 20 minutes are about understanding your situation — your goals, your comfort with risk, your timeline, what financial freedom actually looks like for you. Not a generic version of it. Yours.

  3. I show you one real example.

    Something concrete — how a specific decision might play out, with real numbers, in plain language you can follow.

  4. You decide what's next.

    Maybe that's joining a small community I work alongside. Maybe it's a short resource list and us parting ways. Maybe it's "let's talk again in a month." No sales close. No countdown timer. No upsell at the door.

Who this is for

The honest filter.

You're curious about crypto but the usual rooms feel loud, gendered, or hostile to a real beginner question.

Frequently, gently asked

Questions people actually ask on the phone.

Is this a scam?

Honest answer: I'd be suspicious too. The free call is 30 minutes, on video, with a real person — me. I don't ask for money, card details, or any financial information on the call. If at the end I think there's a community or resource that would suit you, I'll mention it — and that mention is the only thing you'd ever pay for, only if you opted in afterwards. If nothing fits, we part ways and you've lost 30 minutes.

Are you a licensed financial advisor?

No. I'm explicitly not a licensed financial services provider and I don't give regulated financial advice. What I share is personal experience and educational context. Any actual financial decision you make is yours; for regulated advice, speak to a licensed FSP registered with the South African FSCA (or the equivalent regulator in your country).

How much money do I need to have to talk to you?

None, for the call. The call is free. What you choose to do after the call is entirely up to you, and there are paths in that don't require a large starting amount.

I've never used crypto. Is this too early?

It's actually the better time. The conversation is easier when you don't have habits or assumptions to unwind. Come with your questions and nothing else.

What does "managing risk" actually mean here?

It means deciding in advance how much you're willing to lose, how you'll know when something's not working, and what part of your life crypto is and isn't allowed to touch. It's deliberately boring. We'll talk about it on the call.

Are you alone, or do you work with a team?

I work alongside a small group of people who've been navigating crypto markets longer than I have. The call is just with me. If we decide to keep working together after, you'd meet the others gradually — never thrown into a Discord and forgotten.

Is this only for women?

The tone and the people I've mostly worked with skew female, but the conversation is open to anyone who resonates with the "calm, not complicated" framing.
